## Purpose

A Pattern represents a recurring psychological regularity identified from multiple observations collected over time.

Patterns connect individual observations into coherent behavioral, emotional, cognitive, or relational structures.

Unlike observations, patterns describe what repeatedly happens rather than isolated events.

Patterns provide the primary evidence from which psychological hypotheses emerge.

---

## Fundamental Principle

Patterns are discovered.

They are not invented.

Every pattern must be supported by multiple independent observations.

Patterns remain probabilistic and continuously revisable.

---

## Pattern Structure

Each Pattern contains:

- Pattern ID
- Name
- Description
- Category
- Confidence
- Frequency
- Supporting Observations
- Related Patterns
- Related Hypotheses
- First Detected
- Last Updated
- Status

## Pattern Formation

A pattern should emerge only when:

- multiple observations support it;
- observations remain consistent over time;
- evidence exceeds uncertainty;
- competing explanations remain possible.

## Relationship to Observations

Observations answer:

"What happened?"

Patterns answer:

"What keeps happening?"

---

## Relationship to Hypotheses

Patterns do not explain behavior.

They describe recurring regularities.

Hypotheses explain why those regularities may exist.

---

## Closing Principle

Patterns reduce psychological complexity by transforming individual observations into meaningful structures.

They represent recurring evidence rather than psychological conclusions.
