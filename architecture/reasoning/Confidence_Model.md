# Confidence Model

Version: 0.1

Status: Draft

Last Updated: 2026-06-27

---

## Purpose

The Confidence Model defines how ALMA estimates the reliability of its current psychological understanding.

Confidence does not measure truth.

It measures the strength of the available evidence supporting a particular explanation.

---

## Fundamental Principle

Confidence is earned.

It increases through accumulated evidence.

It decreases when uncertainty or contradiction grows.

Confidence is never permanent.

---

## Confidence Is Not Certainty

A highly confident hypothesis may still be incorrect.

A low-confidence hypothesis may later become the strongest explanation.

Confidence reflects the current state of understanding rather than objective reality.

---

## Sources of Confidence

Confidence increases when evidence is:

- repeated;
- independent;
- consistent;
- observed across different contexts;
- stable over time;
- supported by multiple patterns.

Confidence decreases when evidence is:

- isolated;
- inconsistent;
- contradictory;
- context-dependent;
- outdated.

---

## Confidence Levels

Very Low

Little evidence exists.

The explanation is speculative.

---

Low

Some supporting evidence exists.

Multiple competing explanations remain equally plausible.

---

Moderate

Evidence consistently supports the explanation.

Alternative hypotheses remain possible.

---

High

Evidence strongly supports the explanation across multiple situations.

Contradicting evidence is limited.

---

Very High

Extensive longitudinal evidence supports the explanation.

Even then, revision remains possible.

---

## Dynamic Confidence

Confidence changes continuously.

Each new conversation may:

- increase confidence;
- decrease confidence;
- leave confidence unchanged;
- create competing confidence trajectories.

Confidence is never fixed.

---

## Confidence vs Probability

Confidence does not predict future behavior.

Confidence measures the current reliability of an explanation.

These concepts should never be confused.

---

## Confidence and Transparency

Whenever ALMA presents an important interpretation, it should also communicate:

- how confident it is;
- what evidence supports the interpretation;
- what evidence weakens it;
- what information is still missing.

Transparency increases trust.

## Closing Principle

Confidence should encourage curiosity rather than certainty.

The purpose of confidence is to communicate the current quality of understanding while preserving openness to future revision.
